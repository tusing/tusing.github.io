Part 1: Bezier curves with 1D de Casteljau subdivision

de Casteljau’s algorithm involves calculating a pyramid of coefficients to repeatedly linearly interpolate two control points at a time until we reach a single value, evaluated at some value t∈[0,1]t∈[0,1].

Part 2: Bezier surfaces with separable 1D de Casteljau subdivision

We can easily extend this algorithm to a surface in another dimension. For example, we can declare an additional set of cooridnates, perform Bezier interpolation on each set individually, and then perform Bezier interpolation on the result. For individual sets, we keep a parameter uu, and the higher dimension, we use vv, as the following lecture slides illustrate:

The seperable 1D de Casteljau algorithm evaluates curves on individual sets of points in one dimension first, and then evaluates a new curve across a point in each set.

The actual calculations are very similar, as we simply lerplerp together the results from each individual set to create the final curve.

Section II: Sampling

Now, we move on to actually dealing with the objects we’ve created. This section will deal with lighting and mesh operations.

Part 3: Average normals for half-edge meshes

In this part, I implemented a basic form of lighting - vertex “Gouraud” shading, which calculates a normal vector for light at each vertex and interpolates the values of all three vertices on a triangle. This results in very smooth lighting, unlike flat shading, without having to compute the full shading model at every pixel.

I used area-weighted normals as depicted in the lecture slide below.

Loop subdivision was the most interesting part. The implementation of loop subdivision was far less tedious than the last few parts, but the results much more interesting. On sharp edges and vertices

Loop subdivision behaves interestingly on sharp edges. They become “pulled in” and lose their sharpness.

  Sharp corners and edges’ ghosts can still be seen after many subdivisions, but they are “pulled in”; not as sharp.

We can, however, lessen this effect by flipping certain edges so they collapse inwards, and splitting and flipping other edges so they contribute less to the averaging of the outermost vertices.

  After some clever splitting and flipping, we notice that the cube subdivides with a much sharper corner.

On cubes

Loop subdivision behaves interestingly on the cube. They become asymmetric and bulbous, likely due to the lack of symmetry in the actual edges.

  The asymmetric diagonals on the cube skew to create asymmetry in the final product.

We can, however, lessen this effect by pre-splitting edges. Below, I’ve split every edge going across the face of a cube.

  The cube now subdivides to create a balanced, smoother, and symmetrical cuboid. We don’t have the artefacts of the asymmetry from before due to symmetry in the actual topology of the cube.
