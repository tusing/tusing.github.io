Part 1: Ray Generation and Scene Intersection

Ray Generation

Ray generation is a fairly simple process. We model the sensor on our camera. Our camera consists of xx-by-yy pixels in R2R2. For each pixel, we generate a point in [0,1]2[0,1]2, in essence, simulating where a photon might “land” on the camera pixel similarly to the random nature of the phenomenon in the physical world. Once we have this point, we perform a transform to world space (R3R3). The slide below shows our camera sensor in world space and the ray we hope to generate:

We’re given two “corner” vectors for the camera, one on the bottom right and the other on the top left. Transforming from our sample [0,1]2[0,1]2 now becomes much simpler: we simply walk along the difference between the top right and bottom left corner in proportion to the sample we’ve generated.

In essence, for some bottom-left corner (x1,y1)(x1,y1) and some top-right corner (x2,y2)(x2,y2), and some sample (a,b)(a,b), our new xx-coordinate is simply xf=x1+a⋅(x2−x1)xf=x1+a⋅(x2−x1), and our yy-coordinate yfyf is simply yf=y1+b⋅(y2−y1)yf=y1+b⋅(y2−y1).

This is our ray’s direction in camera space - we now apply the transform vector c2w to convert it to world space, giving us the direction vector of our ray. The origin is the position of our camera.

Next, I’ll explain the role primitive intersection plays, both algorithmically and as part of the render pipeline.

Primitive Intersection

Primitive intersection is basically a way for us to tell if our ray “hits” any object in the world. We’ve defined two types of primitives - triangles and spheres - and naively, we test our ray against each primitive according to a certain equation to see if it intersects.

The general idea for intersecting any implicit surface is described in the slide below.

Essentially, we describe our object’s surface with some equation fs(p)=0fs(p)=0 and we know our ray’s equation is simply that of a line (f(o+td)=0f(o+td)=0). Where these intersect describes the time that our ray hits the surface of our object.

There are a few optimizations to be made - the Möller-Trumbore algorithm in particular distills a performant algorithm for ray-triangle intersection, given the barycentric coordinates of a triangle.

This was the algorithm I had implemented for testing ray intersection with triangles. Intersections with our remaining primitive, the sphere, can be tested using the quadratic formula, and we solve for the intersection between both the equation for a ray and the surface of a sphere, as described below.

When generating our ray, we define a valid range of tt values and clip this range as we intersect with more primitives. This essentially discards intersections with primitives further than the closest one.

Part 2: Bounding Volume Hierarchy

Motivation

As explained earlier, testing every ray against every object is a massive waste of time (O(num_rays⋅num_objects)O(num_rays⋅num_objects)). Our goal is to reduce the second factor drastically, perhaps more along the lines of a constant number of objects found via a logarithmic search of the object-space.

Construction

The BVH algorithm given to us accomplishes this phenomenon. The algorithm is simple, forming a sort of binary tree. We construct a bounding box around all of our objects, and a target number of objects to have in our leaf node. We split the first node (the root, the bounding box containing every object) along its largest axis into a left- and right- node. We recurse, performing the same algorithm on the left- and right- children until we have a node with no more than our target number of objects

Intersection

Now, to trace a ray, we simply have to check to see if it intersects with a bounding box. The slides below were particularly helpful in implementing this intersection-check. Essentially, we cast a ray and clip it by each axis.

This reduces the number of objects we test against to a constant value, along with a log(number of objects)log(number of objects) search time to reach our leaf box.

Part 3: Direct Illumination

Now that we can efficiently trace rays, it’s time to work on actually representing light as we see it in the real world. There are two ways that we implement direct lighting, and I describe both below.

Uniform Hemisphere Sampling (UHS)

Using uniform hemisphere sampling, we consider a hemisphere around our point of interest, and pick a random direction in this hemisphere to sample. If the ray intersects anything, we add the intersecting object’s emitted light to a sum. We then average this sum and return.

The algorithm is described in the slide below:

The issue that presents itself is that we are fundamentally unlikely to hit a light-emitting source if we sample uniformly in a hemisphere. In fact, most of what we sample will be non-light-emitting, and thus black, so our image will be relatively noisy as we occasionally hit a light and occasionally hit nothing.

The lecture slide below conveniently illustrates this issue:

Importance Sampling (IS)

Importance sampling aims to fix this issue. Instead of sampling randomly from literally any direction in the hemisphere, we focus our samples on existing area- and point-lights. We accumulate light emitted from these sources into our final estimate, and they won’t be mixed in with random null values from non-emitting sources. In the same amount of time as uniform hemisphere sampling would take on an image, we can construct a far less noisy image using importance samlping.

Part 4: Global Illumination

We’ve implemented “direct lighting”, in which we trace every point of interest to all light sources in the scene. However, objects can also contribute light as light reflects off of them - to create a more realistic rendering, we’d like to capture how light behaves when bouncing off of various objects in the scene.

Implementation

Global illumination works by pushing a ray of light through multiple bounces until it terminates, either due to a probabilistically set factor (our Russian Roulette constant) or until it reaches a maximum bounce parameter. Global illumination is a simple recursive call utilizing the parts we’ve implemented earlier, added with our “zero bounce radiance” (light from light-emitting sources).

Part 5: Adaptive Sampling

The biggest issue when sampling is not having enough light. When we’re in a well-lit area, we can easily converge to a good approximation of light with relatively few samples. But in dim areas, we will have much more variance, as we’ll occasionally sample a lot of light and occasionally not. Adaptive sampling is all about finding a balance - culling uneccessary samples in consistent, low-variance areas and sampling more in noisy, high-variance areas. In essence, adaptive sampling helps us to converge to an image just as clean, but faster.

Implementation

We define a variable I=1.96⋅σ√nI=1.96⋅σn. Note that $I$ is small if the variance is small. We set a confidence/p-value of 0.050.05 and check if I≤0.05⋅μI≤0.05⋅μ. (This p-value is adjustable.) We keep on sampling until the condition is met - intuitively, in areas with consistent lighting, σσ will be smaller and we’ll approach 0.05⋅μ0.05⋅μ more quickly.
